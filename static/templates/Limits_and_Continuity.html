What Is a Function?

Q: How do we formalize relationships between inputs and outputs in a predictable way?


A function is a mathematical rule that assigns exactly one output to each input. It maps elements from one set (the domain) to another set (the codomain), preserving a well-defined, deterministic relationship:

\[ f : X \rightarrow Y \]

That means for every \( x \in X \), there exists a unique value \( f(x) \in Y \). Functions are the backbone of all mathematical modeling — from algebra to algorithms, statistics to machine learning.


In finance, data science, and decision-making, functions help us translate uncertain inputs into measurable outputs. A function of more than one input is called a multivariable function.


Example: Option Pricing as a Function


In quantitative finance, the Black-Scholes formula is a function:

\[ C = f(S, K, T, \sigma, r) \]

where:

  • \( S \): current stock price
  • \( K \): strike price
  • \( T \): time to maturity
  • \( \sigma \): volatility
  • \( r \): risk-free rate
The output \( C \) is the theoretical value of a call option. This function gives us a rule to compute fair prices given a variety of assumptions and parameters.



The chart above shows the output of the Black-Scholes formula as a function of the underlying stock price \( S \), with all other parameters held constant. This is a slice of the full multi-variable function.


In higher dimensions (3+ inputs), we cannot visualize the full shape of a function. But we can still understand it by:

  • Fixing all but one variable and analyzing how the output changes
  • Taking partial derivatives to study sensitivity
  • Plotting cross-sections or slices for intuition
This approach is central to both machine learning (model interpretation and learning) and quant finance (sensitivity analysis, aka "the Greeks").


Functions also appear in data science workflows:

  • Model predictions: where input features map to a predicted label or value
  • Loss functions: measure how wrong a prediction is
  • Transformations: mapping raw data into normalized or structured form

Whether defined by a formula, plotted on a graph, or encoded in software — a function always means: one input, one output.


Functions are the language of relationships. They allow us to model systems, define processes, and build intuition from structure — no matter how complex the domain.

What Is a Limit?

Q: How can we understand how a function behaves near a point — even if it's undefined there?


A limit describes the value a function approaches as its input gets arbitrarily close to a specific point. Limits let us reason about functions at their boundaries, around holes, or near discontinuities — all without needing to plug in the exact value.


Formally, we write:

\[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \]

This means that as \( x \) gets closer and closer to \( a \), the function's output \( f(x) \) gets closer and closer to the number \( L \) — regardless of what happens at exactly \( x = a \). The function may be undefined at \( a \), or even have a different value.


Intuition from Both Sides


A limit exists only if the function approaches the same value from the left and from the right:

\[ \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L \]

This is especially useful when analyzing functions with jumps, holes, or asymptotic behavior — common in piecewise models, pricing functions with thresholds, and in signal analysis.


Epsilon-Delta Definition


The formal definition of a limit is precise — and powerful:

\[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \quad \text{means} \quad \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0 \text{ such that if } 0 < |x - a| < \delta, \text{ then } |f(x) - L| < \epsilon \]

In simple terms: we can make \( f(x) \) as close as we want to \( L \), just by taking \( x \) close enough to \( a \). This is how we define “approaching” in a mathematically rigorous way. The formal definition, though precise, is not necessary for most practical applications.


Example: Limit of a Function at a Removable Discontinuity


Consider the function:

\[ f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \]

This function is undefined at \( x = 1 \), but we can simplify it:

\[ f(x) = \frac{(x - 1)(x + 1)}{x - 1} = x + 1 \quad \text{for } x \neq 1 \]

Now we can evaluate the limit:

\[ \lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 1} (x + 1) = 2 \]


Even though \( f(1) \) is undefined, the limit exists and is equal to 2. This is a removable discontinuity.

One-Sided Limits

In some cases, it’s important to consider what happens to a function as \( x \) approaches a point from only one direction — either from the left or from the right. These are known as one-sided limits, and they give us more precise information about a function’s behavior near discontinuities or sharp turns.


The left-hand limit is written as:


\( \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \)


It tells us what value \( f(x) \) approaches as \( x \) comes closer to \( a \) from values less than \( a \) (from the left).


The right-hand limit is written as:


\( \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) \)


It describes the value \( f(x) \) approaches as \( x \) comes from values greater than \( a \) (from the right).


Why One-Sided Limits Matter


One-sided limits are essential for:

  • Defining piecewise or discontinuous functions
  • Identifying jump discontinuities
  • Determining whether a full (two-sided) limit exists


If the left- and right-hand limits agree, then the full limit exists:


If \( \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L \), then \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \)


Limits That Diverge


Sometimes, one-sided limits do not approach a finite value, but instead grow without bound. In such cases, the function is said to diverge to positive or negative infinity.


Example: Consider the function \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \).


As \( x \to 0^- \), \( f(x) \to -\infty \) — the values decrease without bound.
As \( x \to 0^+ \), \( f(x) \to \infty \) — the values increase without bound.


Since the left- and right-hand limits do not agree (and in fact go in opposite infinite directions), the full limit \( \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x} \) does not exist.


Visual Interpretation


One-sided limits are especially helpful when analyzing vertical asymptotes or discontinuities. They tell us not just that the function "blows up," but also in which direction — a distinction that is essential in calculus and mathematical modeling.


Understanding one-sided limits is a key step in mastering limits, continuity, and the foundational ideas behind derivatives and integrals.

Basics of Evaluating Limits

A limit describes the value that a function approaches as the input (usually \( x \)) gets arbitrarily close to a certain point. It allows us to analyze functions at points where they may not be explicitly defined, and it forms the backbone of calculus concepts like continuity, derivatives, and integrals.


The basic notation is:


\( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \)


This means “the value that \( f(x) \) approaches as \( x \) approaches \( a \),” not necessarily what \( f(x) \) equals at \( x = a \).


1. Direct Substitution


If the function is continuous at \( x = a \), we can evaluate the limit by simply plugging in the value:


\( \lim_{x \to 2} (x^2 + 3) = 2^2 + 3 = 7 \)


This method works when no undefined or indeterminate form (like \( \frac{0}{0} \)) arises.


2. Factoring


If direct substitution gives an indeterminate form like \( \frac{0}{0} \), try factoring and simplifying:


\( \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \)


Factor numerator: \( \frac{(x - 1)(x + 1)}{x - 1} \)
Cancel terms: \( \lim_{x \to 1} (x + 1) = 2 \)


3. Rationalizing


For limits involving square roots or irrational expressions, multiplying by the conjugate can simplify the expression:


\( \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x + 1} - 1}{x} \)


Multiply numerator and denominator by \( \sqrt{x + 1} + 1 \) to eliminate the root.


4. Recognizing Special Limits


Some limits are so common that they’re useful to memorize:


  • \( \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x)}{x} = 1 \)
  • \( \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x^n} = 0 \) for \( n > 0 \)
  • \( \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos(x)}{x^2} = \frac{1}{2} \)

With practice, evaluating limits becomes a flexible toolkit — using substitution when possible, algebra when needed, and logic when nothing else works.

Limits at Infinity

In calculus, we often encounter limits where the input variable grows without bound — that is, where \( x \to \infty \) or \( x \to -\infty \). These are known as limits at infinity, and they help us understand the long-term behavior of functions.


Sometimes we also deal with expressions like \( \frac{1}{\infty} \), which are not numbers in themselves, but symbolic representations of vanishing quantities. For example:


\( \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0 \)


As \( x \) becomes extremely large, the value of \( \frac{1}{x} \) becomes extremely small — approaching zero, but never quite reaching it. This is a fundamental idea in analysis, and helps model decay, saturation, and stabilization in many applied settings.


Common Behaviors as \( x \to \infty \)

  • Rational decay: \( \frac{1}{x^n} \to 0 \) as \( x \to \infty \) for any \( n > 0 \)
  • Polynomial growth: \( x^n \to \infty \) as \( x \to \infty \) for \( n > 0 \)
  • Exponential growth: \( e^x \to \infty \), grows faster than any polynomial
  • Logarithmic growth: \( \ln(x) \to \infty \) but very slowly compared to \( x \) or \( x^2 \)
  • Oscillating functions: Functions like \( \sin(x) \) do not converge, but continue oscillating forever

Example: Rational Decay

Consider:


\( \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^2 + 5}{7x^2 + 1} \)


Divide both numerator and denominator by \( x^2 \) (the highest power in the expression):


\( = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3 + \frac{5}{x^2}}{7 + \frac{1}{x^2}} \)


As \( x \to \infty \), the terms \( \frac{5}{x^2} \) and \( \frac{1}{x^2} \) approach 0. So the limit becomes:


\( = \frac{3}{7} \)


When Limits Diverge

Not all limits at infinity converge to a finite number. If a function grows without bound, the limit is said to diverge to infinity.


Example: \( \lim_{x \to \infty} x^2 = \infty \)


This doesn't mean the function "equals infinity," but rather that it grows beyond any finite bound as \( x \) increases.


Why It Matters

Understanding limits at infinity is key for:

  • Analyzing long-term behavior of models
  • Determining horizontal asymptotes of graphs
  • Simplifying rational and exponential expressions
  • Evaluating improper integrals and infinite series


Mastery of these concepts allows you to quickly spot whether a quantity is vanishing, stabilizing, or diverging — a skill that’s critical in calculus, physics, and applied mathematics.

What Is Continuity?

Q: When is a function smooth and uninterrupted?


In everyday language, a continuous function is one you can draw without lifting your pencil from the paper. Mathematically, continuity ensures that the behavior of a function around a point is predictable and unbroken.


A function \( f(x) \) is said to be continuous at a point \( x = a \) if the following three conditions are all satisfied:


  • The function is defined at \( a \): \( f(a) \) exists
  • The limit exists at that point: \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \) exists
  • The value of the function equals the limit: \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \)

If any of these conditions is not met, the function has a discontinuity at that point.


Definition (Continuous on an Interval):


A function \( f(x) \) is said to be continuous on an interval \( I \) if it is continuous at every point within \( I \). This means there are no sudden jumps, holes, or asymptotes throughout the interval.


Continuity is a key concept in calculus and analysis. It guarantees that small changes in input produce small, controlled changes in output, which is crucial in real-world models like physics, economics, and engineering.


Types of Discontinuities:


Understanding the types of discontinuities helps us classify and analyze where and how a function fails to be continuous.


  • Removable Discontinuity: The limit exists, but the function is either undefined or defined differently at that point.
    Example: \( f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \) has a removable discontinuity at \( x = 1 \).
  • Jump Discontinuity: The left-hand and right-hand limits exist but are not equal.
    Example: A piecewise function like \( f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x < 0 \\ 2 & x \geq 0 \end{cases} \).
  • Infinite Discontinuity: The function approaches infinity near the point.
    Example: \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \) has an infinite discontinuity at \( x = 0 \).

Recognizing these types of discontinuities can help in graph analysis, integration, and limit evaluations.



In summary, continuity at a point means no surprises — the function behaves nicely and predictably. It’s a fundamental building block of calculus, enabling more complex operations like differentiation and integration.

Practice Problems: Limits and Continuity

Problem 1

Let \( f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \). What is \( \lim_{x \to 1} f(x) \)?

First, factor the numerator: \[ f(x) = \frac{(x - 1)(x + 1)}{x - 1} \] For \( x \neq 1 \), we can cancel the \( x - 1 \) terms: \[ f(x) = x + 1 \] Therefore: \[ \lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 1 + 1 = 2 \] Even though \( f(1) \) is undefined, the limit exists.




Problem 2

Let \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & x \neq 2 \\ 5 & x = 2 \end{cases} \] Is the function continuous at \( x = 2 \)?

Step 1: Compute the limit from both sides: \[ \lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 4 \] Step 2: Evaluate the function: \[ f(2) = 5 \] Step 3: Since the limit and function value are not equal, \( f \) is not continuous at \( x = 2 \).




Problem 3

Let \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 - x & x < 1 \\ x^2 & x \geq 1 \end{cases} \] Does \( \lim_{x \to 1} f(x) \) exist?

Left-hand limit: \[ \lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = 1 - 1 = 0 \] Right-hand limit: \[ \lim_{x \to 1^+} f(x) = 1^2 = 1 \] Since the left and right limits are not equal, the two-sided limit does not exist. This is a jump discontinuity.




Problem 4

Let \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x - 3} \). What happens as \( x \to 3 \)?

As \( x \to 3^- \), \( f(x) \to -\infty \)
As \( x \to 3^+ \), \( f(x) \to +\infty \)
Since the function diverges in both directions, it has an infinite discontinuity at \( x = 3 \).


Key Takeaways

  • A function maps each input in its domain to exactly one output — it's the backbone of mathematical modeling.
  • A limit describes the value a function approaches as the input gets close to a specific point, even if the function isn’t defined there.
  • A function is continuous at a point if there’s no gap or jump: the value matches the limit at that point.
  • Discontinuities — whether removable, jump, or infinite — represent breakdowns in smooth behavior and can disrupt analysis or prediction.
  • These concepts form the core of calculus, enabling powerful tools like derivatives, integrals, and continuous probability models.

Mastering functions, limits, and continuity sets the stage for exploring change, uncertainty, and complex systems — from physics to finance.
