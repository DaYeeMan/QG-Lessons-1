Introduction

In probability theory, a joint distribution describes the probability of two (or more) random variables occurring simultaneously. Understanding joint distributions is fundamental for analyzing relationships between variables, which is crucial in quantitative finance and statistics.

Key Concepts & Formulas

  • Joint Probability Mass Function (PMF): For discrete variables X and Y, \( P(X = x, Y = y) \).
  • Joint Probability Density Function (PDF): For continuous variables X and Y, \( f_{X,Y}(x, y) \).
  • Properties:
    • \( \sum_x \sum_y P(X = x, Y = y) = 1 \) (discrete)
    • \( \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) dx dy = 1 \) (continuous)

Worked Example

Suppose X and Y are discrete random variables representing the outcome of rolling two fair dice. The joint PMF is:

\[ P(X = x, Y = y) = \frac{1}{36}, \quad x, y \in \{1,2,3,4,5,6\} \]

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Define the joint PMF for two independent coin tosses. What is the probability that both are heads?

For two independent coin tosses, each outcome (H, H), (H, T), (T, H), (T, T) has probability 1/4. The probability both are heads is 1/4.



Problem 2

Given the joint PDF \( f_{X,Y}(x, y) = 6xy \) for \( 0 < x < 1, 0 < y < 1 \), find \( P(X < 0.5, Y < 0.5) \).

\( P(X < 0.5, Y < 0.5) = \int_0^{0.5} \int_0^{0.5} 6xy\,dy\,dx = 0.1875 \)



Problem 3

Explain the difference between joint and marginal distributions.

The joint distribution gives probabilities for combinations of values of two (or more) variables, while the marginal distribution gives probabilities for one variable regardless of the other(s).
