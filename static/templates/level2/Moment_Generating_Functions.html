Introduction

The moment generating function (MGF) of a random variable provides a way to encode all of its moments (mean, variance, etc.) in a single function. MGFs are useful for characterizing distributions and proving limit theorems.

Key Concepts & Formulas

  • Definition: The MGF of X is
    \[ M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] \]
  • Moments: The nth moment is the nth derivative at t=0:
    \[ E[X^n] = M_X^{(n)}(0) \]
  • Uniqueness: If two random variables have the same MGF (in a neighborhood of 0), they have the same distribution.

Worked Example

Let X be a standard normal random variable. Its MGF is:

\[ M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = e^{t^2/2} \]

The first moment (mean) is 0, and the second moment (variance) is 1.

Problem 1

Find the MGF of a Bernoulli(p) random variable.

\( M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = (1-p) + p e^{t} \)



Problem 2

How can you use the MGF to find the mean and variance of a random variable?

The mean is the first derivative at t=0, the variance is the second derivative at t=0 minus the square of the mean.



Problem 3

Why are MGFs useful for proving the Central Limit Theorem?

MGFs make it easy to show that sums of independent random variables converge in distribution to the normal, by showing their MGFs converge to that of the normal.
