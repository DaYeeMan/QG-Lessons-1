Introduction

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is one of the most important results in probability and statistics. It states that the sum (or average) of a large number of independent, identically distributed random variables tends toward a normal distribution, regardless of the original distribution.

Key Concepts & Formulas

  • Statement: If \( X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n \) are i.i.d. with mean \( \mu \) and variance \( \sigma^2 \), then as \( n \to \infty \):
    \[ \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \to N(0,1) \]
    (converges in distribution).
  • Implication: Sample means are approximately normal for large n, even if the underlying distribution is not normal.

Worked Example

Suppose you repeatedly sample 30 values from a uniform distribution and compute their mean. The distribution of these means will be approximately normal.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

State the Central Limit Theorem in your own words.

The sum or average of many independent, identically distributed random variables is approximately normal, regardless of the original distribution.



Problem 2

Why is the Central Limit Theorem important in statistics?

It allows us to use normal-based inference (confidence intervals, hypothesis tests) even when the data is not normal.



Problem 3

Does the Central Limit Theorem apply to dependent variables?

No, the classical CLT requires independence (or weak dependence under some generalizations).
