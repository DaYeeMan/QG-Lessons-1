Introduction

Interarrival times in a Poisson process are the times between consecutive events. These times follow an exponential distribution, which is a key property that characterizes the Poisson process.

Key Concepts & Formulas

  • Exponential Distribution: The interarrival times T_i are exponentially distributed with parameter λ:
    \[ f_T(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0 \]
  • Memoryless Property: For any s, t > 0:
    \[ P(T > s + t | T > s) = P(T > t) \]
  • Mean and Variance: The mean interarrival time is 1/λ and the variance is 1/λ².
  • Independence: Interarrival times are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.).
  • Sum of Interarrival Times: The sum of n interarrival times follows a gamma distribution with parameters n and λ.

Worked Example

Consider a Poisson process with rate λ = 0.5 events per minute. The probability that the time between two consecutive events is greater than 3 minutes is:

\[ P(T > 3) = e^{-\lambda \cdot 3} = e^{-0.5 \cdot 3} = e^{-1.5} \approx 0.223 \]

This demonstrates the exponential distribution for interarrival times.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

For a Poisson process with rate λ = 2, find the expected time until the third event.

The time until the third event is the sum of three interarrival times, which follows a gamma distribution with mean 3/λ = 1.5.



Problem 2

Explain why the memoryless property is crucial for the Poisson process.

The memoryless property ensures that the time until the next event is independent of how long it has been since the last event, which is a defining characteristic of the Poisson process.



Problem 3

How do you generate interarrival times for simulation?

Generate exponential random variables with mean 1/λ using the inverse transform method: T = -(1/λ) * ln(U), where U is uniform on [0,1].
