Introduction

Mixing times measure how quickly a Markov chain approaches its stationary distribution. This concept is crucial for understanding the efficiency of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods and other applications.

Key Concepts & Formulas

  • Mixing Time: The smallest time t such that for all initial distributions Î¼:
    \[ \| \mu P^t - \pi \|_{TV} \leq \epsilon \]
    where \( \pi \) is the stationary distribution.
  • Total Variation Distance: A measure of distance between probability distributions:
    \[ \| \mu - \nu \|_{TV} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i |\mu_i - \nu_i| \]
  • Spectral Gap: The difference between the largest and second-largest eigenvalues of the transition matrix.
  • Conductance: A measure of how well the chain "mixes" between different parts of the state space.
  • Coupling: A technique for bounding mixing times by coupling two copies of the chain.

Worked Example

Consider a simple random walk on a cycle with n vertices. The mixing time is approximately:

\[ t_{mix}(\epsilon) \approx \frac{n^2}{2\pi^2} \log\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon}\right) \]

This shows that the mixing time grows quadratically with the size of the cycle.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

Compute the mixing time for a simple random walk on a complete graph with n vertices.

For a complete graph, the mixing time is \( O(\log n) \), as the chain mixes very quickly due to the high connectivity.



Problem 2

How does the conductance of a graph relate to its mixing time?

The mixing time is inversely proportional to the conductance. Higher conductance means faster mixing and shorter mixing time.



Problem 3

What is the role of coupling in bounding mixing times?

Coupling provides a way to bound the total variation distance between two copies of the chain, which can be used to estimate mixing times.
