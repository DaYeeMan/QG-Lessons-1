Introduction

The Poisson process is a fundamental stochastic process that models the occurrence of events over time. It is characterized by its memoryless property and is widely used in queueing theory, reliability analysis, and other applications.

Key Concepts & Formulas

  • Poisson Process Definition: A counting process {N(t), t ≥ 0} is a Poisson process with rate λ if:
    \[ P(N(t) = k) = \frac{(\lambda t)^k}{k!} e^{-\lambda t} \]
  • Memoryless Property: The time until the next event is exponentially distributed with mean 1/λ.
  • Independent Increments: The number of events in disjoint time intervals are independent.
  • Stationary Increments: The distribution of N(t+s) - N(s) depends only on t, not on s.
  • Superposition: The sum of independent Poisson processes is also a Poisson process.

Worked Example

Consider a Poisson process with rate λ = 2 events per hour. The probability of exactly 3 events occurring in 2 hours is:

\[ P(N(2) = 3) = \frac{(2 \cdot 2)^3}{3!} e^{-2 \cdot 2} = \frac{64}{6} e^{-4} \approx 0.195 \]

This demonstrates the Poisson distribution for the number of events in a fixed time interval.

Problem 1

For a Poisson process with rate λ = 1.5, find the probability of at least 2 events in 3 hours.

\( P(N(3) \geq 2) = 1 - P(N(3) = 0) - P(N(3) = 1) = 1 - e^{-4.5} - 4.5e^{-4.5} \approx 0.938 \)



Problem 2

Explain the relationship between the Poisson process and the exponential distribution.

The interarrival times in a Poisson process are exponentially distributed with mean 1/λ. This is a consequence of the memoryless property.



Problem 3

How do you simulate a Poisson process?

Generate exponential random variables with mean 1/λ for interarrival times, then cumulatively sum them to get event times.
