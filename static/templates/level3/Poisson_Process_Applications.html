Introduction

Poisson process applications span across various fields including telecommunications, finance, biology, and engineering. The Poisson process provides a robust framework for modeling random events that occur independently over time.

Key Concepts & Formulas

  • Telecommunications: Modeling call arrivals, packet arrivals in networks, and system failures.
  • Finance: Modeling price jumps, default events, and trading activity.
  • Biology: Modeling mutations, cell divisions, and neural spike trains.
  • Engineering: Modeling component failures, maintenance events, and quality control defects.
  • Superposition: The sum of independent Poisson processes is also a Poisson process with rate equal to the sum of individual rates.
  • Thinning: If events in a Poisson process are independently accepted with probability p, the accepted events form a Poisson process with rate λp.

Worked Example

Consider a call center where calls arrive according to a Poisson process with rate λ = 10 calls per hour. The probability of receiving exactly 15 calls in a 2-hour period is:

\[ P(N(2) = 15) = \frac{(10 \cdot 2)^{15}}{15!} e^{-10 \cdot 2} = \frac{20^{15}}{15!} e^{-20} \approx 0.0516 \]

This demonstrates how the Poisson process models real-world arrival patterns.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

In a manufacturing process, defects occur according to a Poisson process with rate 0.1 defects per hour. Find the probability of no defects in an 8-hour shift.

\( P(N(8) = 0) = e^{-0.1 \cdot 8} = e^{-0.8} \approx 0.449 \)



Problem 2

Explain how thinning can be used to model different types of events in a single Poisson process.

Thinning allows us to split a Poisson process into multiple independent Poisson processes by independently accepting or rejecting events based on their properties.



Problem 3

How do you apply the superposition property in practice?

Superposition is used when multiple independent sources contribute to a single arrival process, such as multiple call centers feeding into a central system.
