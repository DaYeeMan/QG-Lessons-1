Introduction

The M/M/1 queue is one of the most fundamental queueing models, representing a single-server queue with Poisson arrivals and exponential service times. The notation M/M/1 means: Markovian arrivals (Poisson), Markovian service times (exponential), and 1 server.

Key Concepts & Formulas

  • Model Assumptions:
    • Arrivals follow a Poisson process with rate λ
    • Service times are exponentially distributed with rate μ
    • Single server with infinite waiting room
    • First-come-first-served (FCFS) discipline
  • Traffic Intensity:
    \[ \rho = \frac{\lambda}{\mu} \]
  • Stability Condition: \(\rho < 1\) (arrival rate must be less than service rate)
  • Stationary Distribution: For \(n \geq 0\):
    \[ \pi_n = (1-\rho)\rho^n \]
  • Performance Measures:
    • Expected number in system: \(L = \frac{\rho}{1-\rho}\)
    • Expected number in queue: \(L_q = \frac{\rho^2}{1-\rho}\)
    • Expected waiting time: \(W = \frac{1}{\mu-\lambda}\)
    • Expected queue time: \(W_q = \frac{\rho}{\mu-\lambda}\)

Worked Example

Consider an M/M/1 queue with arrival rate λ = 4 customers per hour and service rate μ = 5 customers per hour.

Traffic intensity: \(\rho = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8\)

Performance measures:

\[ \begin{align} L &= \frac{0.8}{1-0.8} = 4 \text{ customers} \\ L_q &= \frac{0.8^2}{1-0.8} = 3.2 \text{ customers} \\ W &= \frac{1}{5-4} = 1 \text{ hour} \\ W_q &= \frac{0.8}{5-4} = 0.8 \text{ hours} \end{align} \]

Probability of n customers in system: \(P(n) = 0.2 \cdot 0.8^n\)

Practice Problems

Problem 1

What happens to the queue length as the traffic intensity approaches 1?

As \(\rho \to 1\), the expected queue length \(L = \frac{\rho}{1-\rho} \to \infty\). The system becomes unstable and queues grow without bound.



Problem 2

For an M/M/1 queue with λ = 6 and μ = 8, find the probability of having exactly 2 customers in the system.

\(\rho = \frac{6}{8} = 0.75\), so \(P(2) = (1-0.75)(0.75)^2 = 0.25 \cdot 0.5625 = 0.1406\)



Problem 3

What is the relationship between L, L_q, and the probability that the server is busy?

\(L = L_q + \rho\), where \(\rho\) is the probability the server is busy. This follows from \(L_q\) being the number waiting and \(\rho\) being the probability of 1 customer being served.
